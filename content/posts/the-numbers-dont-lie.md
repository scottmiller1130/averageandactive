I've been tracking my weight through every training phase for the past couple of years. Not obsessively — just consistently enough to see patterns. And the patterns tell a story that's pretty simple: your body follows your training and your fork. That's it.

No magic. No secrets. Just tweaks to diet and training that drove the scale in different directions depending on what I was asking my body to do.

## The Phases

**The Lifting Phase (215-220 lbs)**
This is where it started. Straight strength training, eating in a surplus, focused on getting stronger. Four to five days a week in the garage gym, compound lifts, progressive overload. The scale crept up and so did the numbers on the bar. Body felt solid. Clothes got tighter in the right places.

**Adding Cardio and Races (220 → 205 lbs)**
Then I got curious about endurance. Started adding zone 2 cardio, signed up for my first race, and everything shifted. The combination of increased cardio volume and dialing back calories slightly drove the weight down steadily. My VO2 max climbed. My resting heart rate dropped. I felt lighter on my feet — literally and mentally.

The races were the accelerant. When you have a sprint triathlon on the calendar, you train differently. Long runs, brick workouts, open water swims. That kind of volume burns through calories and the weight came off without me trying to lose it. My body just adapted to what I was asking it to do.

**The Current Muscle Building Phase (205 → 230+ lbs)**
After the racing season, I shifted back to building. More lifting volume, higher calories, prioritizing protein, backing off the cardio. The goal was to put on muscle before tri training kicks off in April. And it worked — I'm north of 230 now, stronger than I've been in a while, and filling out shirts again.

But here's the thing about 230.

## The Hard Part Is Coming

I'm about to start Kokopelli Olympic Triathlon prep in April. And the honest truth is — running at 230-plus sucks. There's no way around it. Every pound is a pound your knees, ankles, and lungs have to carry. The first few weeks of ramping run volume back up are going to be miserable. I know this because I've done it before.

The body will adapt. It always does. As the cardio volume goes up and the training shifts, the weight will start to come down. I'll keep fueling properly — this isn't about starving myself thin. It's about letting the training do what training does. The composition will shift. The scale will move. The endurance will build back.

I'll also keep lifting through all of it. Not at the same volume or intensity as a pure building phase, but enough to maintain as much muscle as possible. The research is clear — you don't have to choose between strength and endurance if you're smart about programming. You just can't maximize both at the same time.

## The Mental Game

This is the part nobody talks about enough. I don't like losing size. I like how I look and feel when I'm 230 and full. Watching the scale drop and seeing shirts fit looser hits different when you worked hard to fill them out.

But I also don't like being gassed on a three-mile run. I don't like my knees aching after a long training day. I don't like showing up to a start line feeling heavy.

It's a trade-off, and it messes with your head in both directions. When I'm big, I want to be leaner. When I'm lean, I want to be bigger. The grass is always greener on the other side of body composition.

The thing that keeps me grounded is the data. The numbers don't care about how I feel — they just show me what's happening. Weight trending down? Training is working. VO2 max improving? Cardio adaptations are kicking in. Lifts holding steady? Muscle preservation is on track. The numbers cut through the noise.

## What I've Learned

Every phase taught me something:

- **Weight follows function.** Train for strength and eat for it, you'll gain. Train for endurance and fuel for it, you'll lean out. Your body is smarter than you think.
- **Small diet tweaks compound.** I never made dramatic calorie swings. A couple hundred calories in either direction, consistently, moved the needle over weeks and months.
- **Cardio and lifting aren't enemies.** They serve different goals. The key is knowing which one is the priority in a given phase and programming accordingly.
- **The scale is one data point.** It matters, but it's not the whole story. How you feel, how you perform, how your clothes fit — all of it matters.
- **Every transition is uncomfortable.** Going from lifting to endurance feels like starting over. Going from endurance to lifting feels sluggish. The in-between is where the mental toughness gets built.

## What's Next

April 1 the Kokopelli training plan starts. The weight will come down. The miles will go up. I'll document the whole thing — the good weeks, the bad weeks, the mornings where I don't want to run, and the days where it clicks.

230 to race weight. Here we go again.
