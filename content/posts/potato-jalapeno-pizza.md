I know what you're thinking. Potatoes on pizza? Carbs on carbs? Yes. And once you try it, you'll wonder why you ever put up with another mediocre pepperoni pie when this exists. This is not a gimmick pizza. This is the one my wife and I make when we actually want to enjoy dinner, and it delivers every single time.

The idea is simple — sourdough crust, paper-thin potatoes, jalapeno olive oil as the sauce, and then you finish it with cold Greek yogurt and fresh chives right when it comes out of the oven. The hot-and-cold contrast is the whole move. Crispy crust, tender potatoes, a little heat from the oil, and then that cool, tangy hit from the yogurt. It just works.

## The Dough

This starts with a sourdough pizza dough, and I'm going to give you the full recipe because the dough matters. You cannot fake this part. The recipe makes four pies — 1000g of bread flour (or a 75/25 split of bread flour and Tipo 00 if you want to get fancy), 700g of room temp water for 70% hydration, 200g of active sourdough starter, 20g of salt, and 20g of olive oil if you want a slightly more tender chew. That's it.

Mix the flour and water, let it autolyse for 45 minutes, then add the starter, salt, and oil. Stretch and fold every 20 to 30 minutes for 4 sets, then let it bulk ferment for 6 to 8 hours until it's about 50% bigger and full of bubbles. Divide into four balls, shape them tight, and cold proof in the fridge for 12 to 24 hours. The cold proof develops flavor and makes the dough way easier to handle the next day.

Pull the dough balls out an hour or two before you're ready to bake. Cold dough fights back when you try to stretch it — give it time to relax.

## The Potatoes

Use a mandoline. I'm serious. You need the potatoes sliced so thin they're almost see-through. Yukon Golds are my go-to because they hold their shape, get tender fast, and have a buttery flavor that plays perfectly with everything else on this pizza. Toss the slices with a little regular olive oil, flaky salt, and black pepper while the dough comes to temperature. That's it for prep — dead simple.

## The Build

Here's where it comes together. Stretch your dough out to about 12 to 14 inches — use your hands, not a rolling pin. Drizzle jalapeno olive oil generously across the surface. This is your sauce. There's no tomato, no white sauce, no pesto. Just the jalapeno oil doing its thing. Spread it around with the back of a spoon and don't be shy with it.

Shingle the potato slices over the whole surface in a single layer, overlapping slightly. Hit them with one more small drizzle of the jalapeno oil and a pinch of flaky salt. That's a complete pizza — launch it.

## Cooking It

I use an Ooni Koda 16 and it's a game changer for pizza at home. Preheat for 20 to 30 minutes until the stone is around 800 to 900 degrees, then bake each pie for about 60 to 90 seconds, rotating every 15 to 20 seconds with a turning peel. You're looking for blistered, leopard-spotted crust and potatoes that are tender with crispy edges.

If you don't have an outdoor pizza oven, you can absolutely do this in a home oven. Crank it to 500 or 550 — as hot as it goes. Put a pizza steel or stone on the middle rack and let it preheat for at least 45 minutes. A pizza steel is better than a stone here because it holds and transfers heat faster. Bake for 6 to 8 minutes, rotating halfway, and hit the broiler for the last 60 to 90 seconds if you want some extra char on top. It won't be identical to an outdoor oven, but it'll still be excellent.

Another option — a cast iron skillet. Heat the skillet on the stove over medium-high, place your stretched dough in it, add your toppings, then transfer to the oven under the broiler for 3 to 4 minutes. This gives you a great crispy bottom from the pan and blistered top from the broiler.

## The Finish

This is the part that takes this pizza from really good to something people remember. As soon as it comes out of the oven, dollop cold Greek yogurt across the top. Full fat, straight from the fridge. Don't spread it — just drop 5 or 6 spoonfuls randomly across the pizza. Then snip fresh chives all over the top.

The hot pizza against the cold yogurt is everything. The yogurt is tangy and cool, the potatoes are salty and crispy, the jalapeno oil has that slow creeping heat, and the sourdough crust ties it all together. Every bite is a little different depending on how much yogurt you hit, and that's part of what makes it fun.

## Just Try It

I know potato pizza sounds like something you'd scroll past. I get it. But this is the pizza I make when I actually want to impress someone — including myself. It's not complicated, it doesn't need a hundred toppings, and it proves that sometimes the best food is the stuff that sounds weird on paper but just works when you eat it. Make the dough tonight, bake tomorrow. You won't regret it.
