This is the recovery tool with the most impressive research behind it. ## Red Light Therapy

Red light therapy, or photobiomodulation, uses specific wavelengths of light (660nm red and 810-850nm near-infrared) that your mitochondria absorb. The research here is promising but still developing. A meta-analysis of 28 studies found overall benefits for muscle performance and recovery. What's interesting is that applying red light before exercise (40-60 minutes prior) seems to reduce muscle damage markers and inflammation. ## Stretching

Here's where the science might surprise you. A 2025 Delphi consensus statement from international researchers concluded that stretching is "largely inefficient as a post-exercise recovery strategy." Slow dynamic stretching, where you move through the stretch at a controlled pace, is showing some of the best results for maintaining mobility without the downsides of long static holds.

During recovery week, I'm doing 15-20 minutes of slow dynamic stretching daily. Not because it's going to fix my injuries overnight, but because maintaining range of motion keeps everything else working properly. I focus on the areas that get tight from training â€” hips, shoulders, and ankles.

## Band Work

Resistance bands are underrated for recovery. The research here is strong, especially for rehabilitation. The beauty of bands during a recovery week is low-load sufficiency. I'm doing light band work daily â€” shoulder rotations, lateral walks, pull-aparts, ankle stability exercises. 15-25 reps, 2-3 sets, nothing that approaches failure. The goal is blood flow, joint stability, and maintaining movement patterns without loading the areas that need rest.
